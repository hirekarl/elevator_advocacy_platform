# Heat Seek — Partnership Strategy

**Last updated:** April 24, 2026
**Status:** Research complete; outreach not yet initiated
**Website:** heatseek.org

---

## Why This Matters

Heat Seek is the closest organizational analog to Elevator Advocate in the NYC civic tech ecosystem. Both platforms:
- Collect building failure data that tenants can use in housing court, rent strikes, and repair negotiations
- Produce timestamped, verified records that are harder to dismiss than 311 complaints
- Partner with community organizing groups rather than operating as standalone consumer apps
- Target the same population: low-income tenants in rent-stabilized South Bronx buildings with negligent landlords

They are not a competitor. They cover **heat** (October–May, hardware-dependent, small-scale). Elevator Advocate covers **vertical mobility** (year-round, software-only, unlimited scale). The buildings with chronic heat violations and the buildings with chronic elevator failures are often the same buildings — the Parkash landlord connection across D27 data and the January 2025 CITY article is one documented example of this pattern.

---

## The Opportunity

**Referral relationship:** Heat Seek deploys sensors in ~50–75 apartments per season, working closely with tenant organizers. Those same tenants live in buildings with elevators. A referral from Heat Seek to Elevator Advocate costs nothing and extends the advocacy infrastructure the tenant already has. The reverse is equally true — tenants who document elevator outages through Elevator Advocate may be living in buildings where Heat Seek's seasonal work would be high-value.

**Shared landlord intelligence:** Both platforms accumulate evidence about the same bad actors. A landlord who allows heat to drop below 62°F at 2am in January is likely the same landlord deferring elevator maintenance. Sharing patterns (not raw tenant data) across platforms could strengthen both orgs' advocacy and legislative arguments.

**Narrative power:** The combination of heat data + elevator data for a single building is a more compelling legislative exhibit than either alone. For a Bronx tenant who is elderly, mobility-impaired, or caring for young children: a broken elevator during a July heat spike is a compounding crisis. That story — documented with Heat Seek temperature logs and Elevator Advocate outage records — is exactly what the Housing & Buildings Committee needs to see.

**Credibility transfer:** Heat Seek is already embedded with CASA and has appeared in the NYC Comptroller's 2025 "Turn Up the Heat" audit. Association with them signals serious civic tech work to community orgs that might otherwise be skeptical of a new platform.

---

## The Ask

This is not a pitch. Heat Seek is a peer, not a prospective user or funder. The approach is a **peer introduction** with an offer of mutual benefit.

The ask, in order of ambition:

1. **Referral exchange** — each org refers tenants to the other when the problem falls outside their coverage (elevator issues → Elevator Advocate; heat issues → Heat Seek).
2. **Co-advocacy on shared buildings** — when both platforms have data on the same building or landlord, coordinate public pressure or legislative briefings.
3. **Joint narrative** — develop one case study (one building, one landlord, both heat and elevator failures documented) that both orgs can use in council briefings, media, and coalition work.

Do not ask them to integrate platforms, share raw tenant data, or co-brand the product. Keep it simple and reversible.

Do not lead with the platform features. Lead with the shared problem and the shared tenant.

**Timing:** If no warm intro by mid-May, send cold outreach before June (before summer elevator complaint peak).

## Talking Points

**What Elevator Advocate does (peer framing):**
"We built a platform that lets tenants document elevator outages in real time — two residents reporting the same status within two hours creates a verified record, timestamped and independent of 311 lag. It generates 311 scripts and council outreach based on the building's full complaint history. Free, mobile-first, English and Spanish."

**Why heat and elevators overlap:**
"The buildings where you're deploying sensors in January are often the same buildings where the elevator is out in July. The Parkash family — your HPD heat case from January 2025 — also owns 89-20 161 Street in Jamaica, Queens, which is D27's top chronic elevator offender by complaint volume. Same landlord behavior pattern, different systems."

**What we're not asking for:**
"We're not asking you to integrate anything or share tenant data. We're asking whether there's a referral relationship that makes sense — you send tenants with elevator problems our way, we send tenants with heat problems yours. And if we ever have data on the same building, we coordinate the narrative before taking it to council."

---

## What Not to Do

- **Don't pitch them as a user.** Heat Seek is an organization, not a tenant. They won't "use" Elevator Advocate; they'll refer tenants to it.
- **Don't lead with technology.** They already have tech. Lead with the shared problem and the shared tenant.
- **Don't conflate the platforms.** They collect continuous sensor data; we collect event-triggered tenant reports. These are different things that do different jobs. Don't oversell integration potential.
- **Don't approach before the CASA relationship opens.** A cold approach to Heat Seek before Karl has any South Bronx org credibility is a harder pitch. Let CASA come first.
